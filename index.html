1. Energy Metabolism and Feeding Control

\

Energy needs are constant for maintenance and brain function (which uses 25% of total energy), but eating is episodic [1].

\ \
\
\

Satiety Signals (Inhibitors)

\
    \
  • CCK: Secreted during fat/protein meals; promotes satiety via the vagus nerve [2, 3].
    • \
  • Amylin: Released with insulin; delays gastric emptying [2].
    • \
  • PYY: Released from the ileum; inhibits NPY/AgRP neurons in the arcuate nucleus [4].
    • \
  • Insulin: The sole hormone responsible for anabolic energy storage as glycogen and fat [5, 6].
    • \
  • Leptin: Proportional to fat mass; suppresses appetite and supports reproduction [7, 8].
    • \
\
\
\

Orexigenic Signals (Promoters)

\
    \
  • Ghrelin: Peaks at scheduled meal times; stimulates NPY and opposes leptin [9].
    • \
  • NPY: Potent stimulator of food seeking/hoarding [10].
    • \
  • AgRP: Antagonizes melanocortin receptors to decrease metabolic expenditure [11].
    • \
  • Orexin: Induces feeding while inhibiting sleep [12].
    • \
\
\
\ \

Disordered Metabolism

\

Obesity is characterized by a height-to-waist ratio exceeding 2:1 and is linked to central insulin insensitivity [13, 14]. Type 2 Diabetes involves failed insulin signaling, leading to hyperglycemia which is toxic to the nervous and circulatory systems [15, 16].

\

2. The Physiology of Stress

\

Stress is a state of threatened homeostasis where the body attempts to re-establish balance through allostasis [17, 18].

\ \
\
\

SAM System (Rapid)

\

The Sympathetic Nervous System triggers the adrenal medulla to release epinephrine and norepinephrine within seconds [19, 20]. This facilitates "fight or flight" by rerouting blood to muscles and the brain [19, 20].

\
\
\

HPA Axis (Slower)

\

The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal axis releases Glucocorticoids (CORT) over minutes [20, 21]. It involves CRH from the hypothalamus and ACTH from the pituitary [20].

\
\
\ \

General Adaptation Syndrome (GAS)

\
    \
  • Alarm: Initial stress response (hypertrophy of adrenals, shrunken thymus) [21, 22].
    • \
  • Resistance: The body adapts and symptoms of the alarm phase disappear [22].
    • \
  • Exhaustion: Prolonged exposure leads to stress pathology and death [22].
    • \
\ \

Pathological Effects of Chronic Stress

\

When the stress response is not terminated efficiently, it leads to allostatic overload [23]. This can cause myopathy, infertility, cardiovascular disease (CVD), and hippocampal atrophy [24, 25].

\

\

3. Developmental Programming & Epigenetics

\

Early life stressors can epigenetically program the brain, altering future coping mechanisms [26, 27].

\ \

The Methylation Mechanism

\

In rat pups, **inattentive maternal care** leads to high methylation of the glucocorticoid receptor (GR) gene, "switching it off" and resulting in a higher stress response in adulthood [27]. Maternal licking and grooming triggers serotonin release that leads to demethylation, allowing for more GR expression and a more relaxed adult [27].

\ \

Prenatal Stress

\

Maternal glucocorticoids can cross the placenta and block male-typical neural sex circuit development by altering fetal testosterone surges [28]. This often leads to impaired male reproductive behaviors in adulthood [28].
